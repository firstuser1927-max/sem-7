Unit 3 — Words & Word Forms

📚 Unit 3 of 5 ⏱ 7 Hours 🎯 14% Weightage 📅 Sem 7 · GTU
🤔 Why Does This Unit Matter?

How does Google know king − man + woman = queen? How does a search engine know automobiles is relevant to cars even though the words are different?

Words must be converted into smart numbers (vectors) that capture meaning and relationships — not just counts. This unit shows you exactly how.

1 Bag of Words (BoW)

Definition

A Bag of Words model represents a document as an unordered collection of word counts, completely ignoring grammar and word order. Think of tipping a bag of scrabble tiles — only which tiles (words) are present matters, not their arrangement.

Step-by-Step Construction

  1. Build Vocabulary — collect every unique word across all documents.
  2. Count Vector Representation — for each document, record how many times each vocabulary word appears. Words absent = 0.

Worked Example

Consider three documents:

  • D1: "I love NLP"
  • D2: "I love cricket"
  • D3: "NLP is amazing"

Vocabulary: {I, love, NLP, cricket, is, amazing}

DocumentIloveNLPcricketisamazing
D1111000
D2110100
D3001011

BoW Pipeline for Text Classification

A classic 7-step pipeline (frequently asked in GTU — W23, 7 marks):

Raw Text→ Normalize→ Tokenize→ Remove Stopwords→ Build Vocabulary→ Count Vectors→ ML Classifier
flowchart LR A["Raw Text"] --> B["Normalize\n(lowercase, strip)"] B --> C["Tokenize\n(split words)"] C --> D["Remove\nStopwords"] D --> E["Build\nVocabulary"] E --> F["Count\nVectors"] F --> G["ML Classifier\n(Naïve Bayes, SVM…)"] style G fill:#4f46e5,color:#fff

Limitations of BoW

  1. Ignores word order — "Dog bites man" and "Man bites dog" produce identical vectors.
  2. High dimensionality — vocabulary of 50,000 words → 50,000-dimensional sparse vectors.
  3. No semantic meaning — "car" and "automobile" are treated as completely different features.
  4. Frequent words dominate — "the", "is", "a" score high unless removed (TF-IDF partially fixes this).

2 Skip-gram

Definition

Skip-gram is a neural network trained to predict surrounding context words from a given center word. The network learns rich word representations (embeddings) as a by-product of this prediction task.

💡 Intuition: "You shall know a word by the company it keeps." — J.R. Firth, 1957.
center word → predict neighbours

Architecture

flowchart TD CW["Center Word\n(One-hot Input)"] H["Hidden Layer\n(Embedding — e.g. 300-dim)"] C1["Context Word C₁"] C2["Context Word C₂"] C3["Context Word C₃"] CW --> H H --> C1 H --> C2 H --> C3 style H fill:#4f46e5,color:#fff style CW fill:#eef2ff style C1 fill:#f0fdf4 style C2 fill:#f0fdf4 style C3 fill:#f0fdf4

Training Process

  1. Slide a window of size w across the corpus sentence by sentence.
  2. The center word becomes the input (one-hot encoded).
  3. Every word within the window is a target context word.
  4. Backpropagation nudges the hidden-layer weights so context words score high; all other words score low.
  5. After training, the hidden-layer weight matrix rows are the word embeddings.

Worked Example — Window = 2

Sentence: "I love studying NLP deeply"

Center WordContext Words (window = 2)
Ilove, studying
loveI, studying, NLP
studyingI, love, NLP, deeply
NLPlove, studying, deeply
deeplystudying, NLP

Skip-gram with Negative Sampling

Standard Skip-gram must update weights for every word in the vocabulary on each training step — very slow for a 50,000-word vocabulary. Negative Sampling is the fix:

  • For each positive (center, context) pair, randomly sample k negative (non-context) words.
  • Only update weights for k + 1 output nodes instead of all 50,000.
  • Typical k = 5–20. Training speed improves dramatically with negligible quality loss.

3 CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)

Definition

CBOW is the mirror image of Skip-gram. Instead of predicting context from the center word, CBOW predicts the center word from the averaged context words.

4 Embedding Representations for Words

Definition

A word embedding is a dense, low-dimensional, real-valued vector that places semantically similar words close together in vector space. Unlike BoW (sparse, high-dimensional), embeddings are compact and meaning-aware.

BoW vs Word Embeddings

FeatureBoWWord Embeddings
SizeVocabulary-sized (sparse)Fixed small dim (e.g. 300)
Semantic Meaning❌ None✅ Captured
Word Order❌ Ignored✅ Partially captured
Arithmetic❌ Meaningless✅ king − man + woman ≈ queen

The Magic: Vector Arithmetic

Embeddings encode relationships as directions in space:

king − man + woman ≈ queen
Paris − France + India ≈ Delhi

The difference vector (man → woman) represents gender. Adding it to "king" navigates to "queen". Similarly, (France → India) represents country shift.

Word2Vec (Google, 2013)

The landmark paper by Mikolov et al. introduced Word2Vec — a framework that trains either Skip-gram or CBOW to produce one fixed-size vector per word. Pre-trained on Google News corpus (100 billion words).

Major Embedding Models

ModelYearByKey Innovation
Word2Vec2013GoogleSkip-gram / CBOW; efficient neural embeddings
GloVe2014StanfordGlobal co-occurrence statistics + local context
FastText2016FacebookSubword (character n-gram) embeddings; handles OOV words
BERT2018GoogleContextual embeddings; same word → different vectors per context
💡 Key Limitation of Word2Vec / GloVe
Each word gets one fixed vector regardless of context. "bank" in "river bank" and "bank account" get the same vector. BERT fixes this with contextual embeddings.

5 Lexical Semantics

Definition

Lexical Semantics is the branch of linguistics that studies word meanings and the relationships between words. It provides the theoretical foundation for knowledge-based NLP.

Key Semantic Relationships

RelationSymbolDescriptionExample
Synonymy≈Same / similar meaningcar ≈ automobile
Antonymy↔Opposite meaninghot ↔ cold
Polysemy—One word, multiple related meanings"bank" — financial institution / river bank
Homonymy—Same spelling, unrelated meanings"bat" — flying animal / cricket bat
Hyponymy (IS-A)⊆A is a type of Bpoodle IS-A dog IS-A animal
Meronymy (PART-OF)∈A is a part of Bwheel PART-OF car
⚠️ Polysemy vs Homonymy — The Tricky Distinction
Polysemy: senses are etymologically related ("bank" = riverbank → financial bank, both involve deposit/holding).
Homonymy: same spelling, completely unrelated origin ("bat" the animal and "bat" the wooden stick are coincidentally spelled the same).

WordNet

WordNet is a large lexical database of English, organized hierarchically. Instead of alphabetical ordering, words are grouped by meaning.

TermDefinitionExample
SynsetA set of synonymous words expressing the same concept{ car, auto, automobile, machine, motorcar }
GlossA short textual definition of a synset"a motor vehicle with four wheels; usually propelled by…"

6 Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD)

Definition

Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD) is the task of automatically determining which sense of an ambiguous word is intended given its surrounding context.

Motivating Example — "bank"

  • Sense 1 "She deposited money in the bank." → financial institution
  • Sense 2 "He fished from the river bank." → side of a river

Why is WSD Hard? (3 Reasons)

  1. High Ambiguity: Common words like "run", "bank", "light" can have 20+ senses. Automatically selecting the right one is non-trivial.
  2. Context Sensitivity: The correct sense depends on subtle cues — domain, topic, even tone — requiring deep language understanding.
  3. Knowledge Acquisition Bottleneck: Building comprehensive sense-labelled corpora or knowledge bases is expensive, slow, and language-specific.

7 Knowledge-Based WSD

Core Idea

Uses an external knowledge base (typically WordNet) rather than labelled training data. No annotation effort needed — works out-of-the-box for any word in the dictionary.

The Lesk Algorithm

The Lesk Algorithm (1986) is the classic knowledge-based WSD method:

  1. For each possible sense of the ambiguous word, retrieve its gloss (definition) from WordNet.
  2. Count the number of overlapping words between the gloss and the surrounding context sentence.
  3. The sense whose gloss has the most overlaps wins — assign that sense.

Worked Example

Sentence: "I deposited money in the bank."

Context words: {I, deposited, money, in, the}

SenseWordNet Gloss (simplified)Overlaps with ContextScore
Sense 1 "a financial institution where you can deposit or withdraw money" deposited ✓, money ✓ 2
Sense 2 "sloping land beside a body of water; a river bank" (none) 0
✅ Winner: Sense 1 — Financial Institution (Score = 2)
The gloss for Sense 1 contains "deposit" and "money" which appear in the context. Sense 2 has zero overlaps.

Advantages & Disadvantages

AdvantagesDisadvantages
✅ No training data required❌ Lower accuracy than supervised methods
✅ Works for any word in WordNet❌ Depends on gloss quality and coverage
✅ Transparent and interpretable❌ Glosses are often short → sparse overlaps
✅ Language-portable if WordNet exists❌ Does not adapt to domain-specific usage

8 Supervised WSD

Core Idea

Uses a manually labelled corpus where every ambiguous word occurrence is tagged with its correct sense. A machine learning classifier then learns to predict senses from contextual features.

How It Works — 4 Steps

  1. Collect Labelled Corpus — human annotators tag each occurrence of the target word with the correct WordNet sense.
  2. Extract Features — for each occurrence, extract: surrounding words (± 5 window), POS tags, collocations, syntactic relations.
  3. Train Classifier — train SVM, Naïve Bayes, or neural classifier to map features → sense label.
  4. Predict at Test Time — extract same features from unseen sentence, classifier outputs the predicted sense.

Worked Example — "bank"

Training SentenceKey Nearby WordsLabel
"She went to the bank to withdraw cash."withdraw, cashSense 1 (Financial)
"The bank offered 6% interest."interest, offeredSense 1 (Financial)
"We sat on the grassy river bank."river, grassySense 2 (Riverbank)
"Floods eroded the bank overnight."floods, erodedSense 2 (Riverbank)

Pattern learned: river / water / grassy nearby → Sense 2; money / deposit / interest nearby → Sense 1.
